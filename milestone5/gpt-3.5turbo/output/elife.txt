Proteases encoded by various viruses cleave and disrupt NINL function in a host-specific manner. High-throughput transgenesis using synthetic DNA libraries has been a valuable tool for exploring genetic function, but has been limited to single-cell models. To overcome this restriction, a new approach called Transgenic Arrays Resulting in Diversity of Integrated Sequences (TARDIS) has been developed, allowing for large-scale transgenesis in multicellular systems. TARDIS involves creating individuals carrying experimental sequence libraries, then extracting and integrating individual sequences from these libraries into engineered genomic sites, resulting in thousands of genetically unique transgenic individuals. This system has been successfully demonstrated in Caenorhabditis elegans, significantly increasing transformation yields compared to current methods. TARDIS allows for the creation of uniquely barcoded lineages and transcriptional reporter lines from predefined promoter libraries, providing a powerful tool for the exploration of genetic function in multicellular organisms. By separating and reordering the steps of transgenesis, TARDIS greatly enhances throughput and diversity, making it adaptable to various systems where heritable DNA elements can be introduced with high diversity. The method has been tested extensively in C. elegans, proving its efficiency and versatility, with potential applications in other organisms where high-throughput transgenesis is desired. The design of TARDIS landing pads for introducing barcode fragments and selection of correct integrations has been carefully considered, utilizing split-selection and hygromycin resistance for efficient integration. The system allows for the generation of large, heritable barcode libraries, demonstrating high levels of diversity and unique barcode sequences. Integration frequencies from the array to subsequent generations show a correlation with the barcode frequencies in the original library, with most sequences being incorporated only once. The method has also been successfully used to integrate large promoters into multiple genomic locations simultaneously, offering a tool for precise genetic manipulations across the genome. Overall, TARDIS represents a significant advancement in the field of transgenesis, enabling high-throughput exploratory studies in multicellular organisms that were previously limited by low transformation yields and labor-intensive methods.

Midbrain dopamine (DA) neurons play a crucial role in regulating basal ganglia functions, with complex axonal domains containing non-synaptic release sites and synaptic terminals releasing DA, glutamate, or GABA. The molecular mechanisms governing the connectivity and neurotransmission of DA neurons are unclear. Recent studies suggest that neuroligins, trans-synaptic cell adhesion molecules, influence DA neuron connectivity and function. Deleting all Nrxns in DA neurons in mice led to impaired locomotor response to psychostimulants, alterations in DA neurotransmission, reduced activity-dependent DA release, and increased GABA co-release from DA neuron axons. Nrxns modulated DA neuron connectivity in the ventral tegmental area (VTA) and substantia nigra pars compacta (SNc), affecting motor function and psychostimulant-induced locomotion. Nrxns are crucial for regulating neurotransmission and connectivity of DA neurons, affecting the behavioral response to amphetamine. Nrxn mRNA levels were differentially expressed in VTA and SNc DA neurons, highlighting the specific roles of Nrxn isoforms in different subpopulations. These findings shed light on the role of Nrxns in DA neuron function and neurotransmission, influencing motor behavior, amphetamine response, and synaptic plasticity. Further research is needed to understand the intricate mechanisms underlying Nrxn-mediated modulation of DA neurons and their impact on brain function.
